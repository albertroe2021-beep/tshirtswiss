Fabric Types and Benefits for Workwear

Fabric choice should be viewed from a durability, climate and workplace-suitability perspective. Workwear should support movement, comfort, wash performance and the actual environment where it will be worn.

Heavy Cotton

Comfortable, breathable and robust for t-shirts and basic work garments where natural fibre feel is preferred.

Poly-Cotton Blends

Durable, stable and easier to care for than pure cotton. Useful for repeat uniform programmes and frequent washing.

Performance Polyester

Lightweight and quick drying for warm climates, active field teams and roles where moisture management matters.

Fleece and Brushed Knits

Useful for hoodies and sweatshirts in cooler environments, warehouses, workshops or morning outdoor work.

Softshell Fabrics

Good for lightweight jackets where wind resistance, mobility and a professional outer layer are important.

Bright Colour Options

Bright colour choices can support team visibility and role clarity where appropriate.

Workplace Suitability

Workwear Should Support the Job Being Done

Uniform choices can support day-to-day workplace planning by improving comfort, movement, role identification and suitability for the work environment. Apparel should be selected around the actual tasks performed, not only the company logo.

  • Breathable fabrics are useful for hot outdoor work and physically active roles.
  • Stretch or ergonomic fits can improve movement for bending, lifting and reaching.
  • Durable seams and stronger fabric choices help garments handle repeated wear.
  • Visible colours may be relevant for certain team environments.
  • Layering options help teams adapt to variable weather and indoor-outdoor work.
  • Workplace-specific requirements should be confirmed before ordering.

Procurement Planning

Trade Uniform Programmes Should Be Practical to Manage

Construction and trade teams often reorder as new workers join, sites change or garments wear out. A good workwear programme should make replacement and distribution simple.

  • Define garments by role: site crew, supervisor, office, field service or warehouse.
  • Plan colours for visibility, practicality and brand consistency.
  • Choose branding methods suitable for washing, abrasion and daily use.
  • Document fabric, sizing, logo placement and packing details.
  • Keep extra stock for new starters and replacement garments.
  • Use crew, branch or site packing when uniforms need to be distributed quickly.
